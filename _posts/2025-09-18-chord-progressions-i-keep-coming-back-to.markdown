Not a theory lesson. Just a list of progressions that keep showing up in things I make, with notes on why they work for me.

Roman numeral notation throughout. Uppercase = major chord, lowercase = minor.

---

## i — VI — III — VII (natural minor)

The workhorse. Works in basically any tempo and genre. The VII gives it a slightly unresolved, forward-moving quality — good for verses that need to keep building.

In A minor: `Am — F — C — G`

> The reason this sounds so natural is that all four chords come from the natural minor scale. Nothing is borrowed. It's harmonically coherent without needing any justification.

---

## I — V — vi — III (major)

The major counterpart. Slightly more hopeful. The III at the end is what makes it interesting — it points back toward I but also hints at the relative minor, giving it some ambiguity.

In C major: `C — G — Am — E`

The E major chord (III) is borrowed from the parallel harmonic minor. That raised third in the chord creates a leading tone into A minor, which is why it sounds like it wants to keep going.

---

## i — iv — VII — III — VI — III — VII — i

A full descending-fifths sequence in minor. Sounds inevitable, almost mathematical. Good for slow builds or endings where you want something that feels conclusive without being triumphant.

In D minor: `Dm — Gm — C — F — Bb — F — C — Dm`

Each chord is a fifth below the previous one (or a fourth above, same thing). The sequence wraps back to the tonic without ever feeling forced.

---

## ii — V — I (jazz)

Unavoidable. Once you hear it, you hear it everywhere.

In C major: `Dm7 — G7 — Cmaj7`

The tension is in the G7 — specifically the tritone between the third (B) and seventh (F). That interval wants to resolve: B up to C, F down to E. The I chord satisfies both pulls simultaneously.

| Chord  | Notes         | Function           |
|--------|---------------|--------------------|
| Dm7    | D F A C       | Pre-dominant       |
| G7     | G B D F       | Dominant (tension) |
| Cmaj7  | C E G B       | Tonic (resolution) |

---

## I — bVII — IV (borrowed)

The bVII is borrowed from the parallel minor. Has a rock/modal quality. Works equally well with power chords and full voicings.

In E major: `E — D — A`

The D major chord doesn't belong to the key of E major (where the VII would be D#m), but it's so common that most listeners don't notice or care. The motion from D back to A and then to E feels natural despite the theoretical borrowing.

---

## Practical Notes

A few things I've learned from actually using these:

1. **Context matters more than the progression.** The same four chords sound completely different at 70 BPM versus 140 BPM, with a piano versus a distorted guitar.
2. **Voice leading is half the work.** How you move from one chord to the next — which notes go up, which go down — shapes the feel more than the chord names do.
3. **Repetition earns variation.** Establish a progression, let the listener settle into it, *then* break it. A deviation only lands if there's something to deviate from.

---

None of these are original discoveries. But there is something useful about keeping a short personal list of things you know work, rather than chasing novelty every session.
